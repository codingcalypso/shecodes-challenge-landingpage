History

The name Oklahoma is taken from Choctaw Indian language that means "red people". It is derived from "okla" which means people, and "humma" which means red. The history of Oklahoma has a dark beginning with this land being the home of many Native American tribes, many of whom were forcibly relocated to Oklahoma. This was originally supposed to be considered "Indian Territory" in the 1800's, but ranchers from Texas pushed into the land in search of new farming settlements, taking land away from Native Americans. They did so illegally, as the lands were not open for settlement. These individuals crossed the border "sooner" than they were allowed to, thus starting the nickname of Oklahoma as the "Sooner" State. Oklahoma officially became a state in 1907.

While the Native American population was forced into decline, Oklahoma still has third largest Native American population in the United States and 40% of the state is still categorized as Native American land. There is no way to describe Oklahoma without mentioning the importance and legacy Native Americans have on our state.

Throughout the century, Oklahoma has made a name for itself through oil and natural gas and has allowed the state to thrive economically. The state has now grown to a population just shy of 4 million, and has grown the 2 major cities of Oklahoma City and Tulsa into popular travel cities.
